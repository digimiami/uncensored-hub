# The Goddess in World Religions — Suppressed Feminine Divine

Before there were gods of thunder and judgment, there was the Goddess. In the oldest archaeological records, the divine was overwhelmingly female, rooted in the mysteries of birth, fertility, death, and rebirth. Yet across the sweep of history, the feminine face of the sacred was systematically dethroned, demonized, and erased. This article traces the journey of the Goddess — from her prehistoric ubiquity, through her suppression by patriarchal religions, to her quiet resurgence in the modern world.

## The Ancient Goddess: Prehistoric to Classical

Long before written scriptures, humanity carved its first images of the divine out of stone, bone, and clay — and they were all female. These figurines, often called “Venus” statuettes, speak to a worldview radically different from the one we inherit today.

### The Paleolithic Venus

The **Venus of Willendorf** (c. 28,000–25,000 BCE) and hundreds of similar figures found from Europe to Siberia depict a full-breasted, wide-hipped female form, often faceless, emphasizing the life-giving body. Archaeologists largely agree that these were not erotic objects but sacred representations of the Great Mother — a symbol of fertility, abundance, and the continuity of life. In the absence of male god imagery, it is plausible that prehistoric societies envisioned the divine as fundamentally feminine.

### Inanna and Ishtar

With the rise of urban civilization in Mesopotamia, the Goddess took on complex personalities. **Inanna** (Sumerian) and her later counterpart **Ishtar** (Akkadian) were goddesses of love, war, political power, and the planet Venus. They descended to the underworld, died, and were reborn — a motif of cyclical regeneration. The *Epic of Gilgamesh* and numerous hymns reveal that Ishtar’s temples were economic and ritual centers, staffed by priestesses, sacred prostitutes, and transgendered clergy. Here the feminine divine was not a passive mother but a force of nature, both life-giving and destructive.

### Isis and the Great Mother

In the Nile Valley, the goddess **Isis** became the most widely worshipped deity in the Mediterranean world. Her cult spread from Egypt to Rome, Greece, and beyond, absorbing the attributes of earlier goddesses. Isis was the throne goddess, the magician-healer, the faithful wife who resurrected Osiris, and the protective mother of Horus. Apuleius, in the 2nd century CE, called her “the universal mother, mistress of all the elements, the initial progeny of the ages, supreme among divinities.” Her worship promised personal salvation, a direct relationship with the divine feminine — a threat to the rising monotheisms.

## The Rise of Patriarchy and the Demotion of Goddesses

How did a world saturated with goddess imagery become a world of male gods, prophets, and priests? The shift was neither sudden nor universal, but a series of cultural, military, and ideological revolutions.

### The Shift to Male Sky Gods

Pastoralist and warrior societies, particularly the Indo-Europeans and Semitic tribes, brought with them sky gods — Zeus, Yahweh, Marduk — who asserted dominance over earth goddesses. Creation myths were rewritten. In the Babylonian *Enuma Elish*, the hero-god Marduk defeats the sea-goddess Tiamat, splitting her body to form the heavens and the earth. This narrative of a male god slaying a female chaos monster is a mythological blueprint for patriarchal takeover: order is established by violently subjugating the feminine.

### Demonization of the Serpent and the Sacred Feminine

The serpent, once a universal symbol of the Goddess — associated with wisdom, healing, and the cycle of life — was systematically demonized. In the Garden of Eden, the talking serpent tempts Eve, who then tempts Adam, leading to the Fall. The woman is blamed for humanity’s exile from paradise, and her punishment is subjugation to man. The Genesis story reinterprets an older Near Eastern myth of the goddess and the tree of life, turning it into a weapon against female spiritual authority. The serpent became the Devil, and the wise woman became the witch.

## Suppressed Goddesses in Major World Religions

Every major religion today carries the shadow of the suppressed feminine divine. In some, the goddess is hidden in plain sight; in others, she survives only in folklore, heresy, or mystical undercurrents.

### Hinduism and the Shakti Paradox

Hinduism is often cited as a religion that still honors the Goddess, and indeed, **Devi**, **Durga**, **Kali**, **Lakshmi**, and **Saraswati** are worshipped by millions. However, this is not a simple story of preservation. The great goddess tradition of **Shakti** — the feminine creative power — was often subordinated to the male godhead (Shiva, Vishnu). Kali’s wild, independent power was tamed through marriage to Shiva. The Brahminical orthodoxy systematically sidelined goddess-centered Tantric practices, dismissing them as heterodox or dangerous. In many villages, the grama-devi (village goddess) is still served by low-caste priests, and her worship is often considered “unclean” by the upper-caste mainstream.

### Buddhism and the Feminine Principle

Early Buddhism was a monastic movement that viewed the female body as a hindrance to enlightenment. The Buddha is said to have reluctantly admitted women into the sangha, but with strict subordination. Yet the feminine divine crept back in Mahayana and Vajrayana Buddhism. **Tara**, the “mother of liberation,” is a fully enlightened female Buddha. **Prajnaparamita**, the perfection of wisdom, is personified as a goddess. In Tibetan Buddhism, the union of male and female deities (yab-yum) represents the non-dual nature of reality. Still, the institutional hierarchy of Buddhist monasteries remained overwhelmingly male, and the feminine principle was often an abstraction rather than a lived equality.

### Judaism and the Shekinah

In the Hebrew Bible, God is unambiguously male — a king, father, and warrior. Yet beneath the text, the feminine divine persists. The **Shekinah**, a term from rabbinic literature, refers to the indwelling presence of God, depicted as a feminine entity. In the Kabbalah, the Shekinah is the lowest sefirah (Malkhut), the divine feminine that is exiled from the male Godhead and must be reunited through human righteousness. She is the Sabbath Bride, the Queen, the Mother. The medieval Kabbalists saw the Shekinah in the biblical Song of Songs, interpreting it as a mystical love story between the Jewish people and the feminine divine. But this was esoteric knowledge, confined to male scholars; the public face of Judaism remained strictly patriarchal.

### Christianity and the Virgin Mary as a Suppressed Goddess

The Virgin Mary is the most obvious remnant of the Goddess in Christianity. She is the Theotokos (God-bearer), Queen of Heaven, and Mother of Mercy — titles that echo those of Isis, Astarte, and Artemis. At Ephesus, where the cult of Artemis was once the wonder of the world, the Church Council of 431 CE declared Mary the Mother of God, effectively transferring the goddess’s worship to a Christian figure. Yet Mary was stripped of her sexuality, her autonomy, and her divinity. She is not a goddess but a humble handmaid, “blessed among women” only because she submitted to God’s will. The Gnostic Christians, who venerated the feminine aspect of the divine (Sophia, the Holy Spirit as mother), were declared heretics and their texts destroyed. The suppression of Mary Magdalene as a spiritual leader further erased the feminine apostolic voice.

### Islam and the Pre-Islamic Goddesses

In pre-Islamic Arabia, the Kaaba in Mecca housed hundreds of idols, including three powerful goddesses: **Al-Lat**, **Al-Uzza**, and **Manat**. They were considered daughters of the high god Allah, and intercessors. The Qur’an explicitly mentions them in Surah 53:19–20, and the so-called “Satanic Verses” episode suggests that the Prophet Muhammad at one point acknowledged their intercessory power before retracting it. Islam’s radical monotheism purged the goddesses entirely, replacing them with the singular, genderless (though linguistically male) Allah. The feminine divine was erased from the Arabian Peninsula, surviving only in folk practices and Sufi mysticism, where the female saint (waliya) and the concept of divine love as a female beloved (Layla) faintly echo the old goddess.

## The Witch Hunts and the Erasure of the Divine Feminine

The suppression of the Goddess was not merely a theological project; it was enforced through centuries of violence against women who embodied her power.

### The Burning Times

Between the 15th and 18th centuries, an estimated 40,000 to 100,000 people — roughly 80% of them women — were executed for witchcraft in Europe and colonial America. The Malleus Maleficarum (1486) explicitly linked witchcraft to female sexuality, claiming that women were more carnal and therefore more susceptible to the Devil. The midwife, the herbalist, the solitary woman who lived outside male control — these were the targets. The witch trials systematically destroyed the remnants of pre-Christian goddess worship, folk healing, and female spiritual leadership. Women who had once been the village wise women were now servants of Satan.

### The Inquisition’s Targeting of Female Healers

The female healers of the Middle Ages, who carried on the tradition of the goddess-as-healer, were slowly pushed out of medicine by the male-dominated university system. The Church declared that only priests (male) could channel divine healing. Midwives were particularly suspect because they controlled the mysteries of birth, a domain once sacred to the Goddess. The suppression of women’s knowledge of herbs, contraception, and childbirth was a direct assault on the feminine divine’s last strongholds in daily life.

## Modern Resurgence of the Goddess

The Goddess did not die; she went underground, into fairy tales, folk customs, and the subconscious. In the twentieth and twenty-first centuries, she has returned with striking force.

### Feminist Theology and Wicca

The feminist movement of the 1960s and 1970s sparked a re-examination of the divine feminine. Scholars like Marija Gimbutas (archaeology), Merlin Stone (*When God Was a Woman*), and Carol P. Christ (theology) argued that patriarchy was a historical imposition, not a natural order. Wicca, a modern pagan religion, explicitly centers the Goddess and the God, reviving rituals and symbolism of the ancient feminine divine. The Goddess movement, though diverse, shares a common thread: reclaiming the sacredness of female embodiment, sexuality, and spiritual authority.

### The Return of the Goddess in Popular Culture

The feminine divine has also resurfaced in popular culture, from the mythological narratives of *Wonder Woman* (an Amazon princess) to the worship of figures like Beyoncé’s “Mother Earth” persona. Yoga and meditation practices often invoke the goddesses Kali, Lakshmi, and Saraswati. The archetype of the “wild woman” (Clarissa Pinkola Estés) and the “divine feminine” now permeates wellness culture, though often in a commercialized form. While this resurgence is a far cry from the temple-centered worship of ancient Sumer, it signals a deep cultural hunger for what was lost.

## Conclusion

The Goddess in world religions is a story of presence, suppression, and re-emergence. From the Venus of Willendorf to the Shekinah, from Isis to Mary, the feminine divine has been continuously reshaped, marginalized, and revived. The suppression was not a natural evolution but a deliberate project of patriarchy to control spiritual power and social order. Understanding this history is not just an academic exercise — it is a recovery of a lost inheritance that belongs to all humanity. The Goddess is not a relic; she is an archetype that refuses to die.
